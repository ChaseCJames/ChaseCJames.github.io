About

Background

I grew up near Puget Sound in Seattle, Washington, where tide pooling, looking out for orcas on ferry rides, and watching my grandpa fish for salmon sparked an early fascination with the ocean. That curiosity eventually became a career in marine science. I completed my B.S. in Biology and Ph.D. in Oceanography at UC San Diego’s Scripps Institution of Oceanography, where I developed an integrative, quantitative approach to studying marine ecosystems.

My work sits at the intersection of microbial ecology, community ecology, and biogeography. I am interested in how nutrient supply, physical oceanography, and ecological interactions shape the structure and function of marine microbial communities across space and time. At USC, I work in the Levine Lab where I am developing EcoTRACE, a trait-based phytoplankton population dynamics model built on a quota framework and explicitly capable of stochastic evolution. EcoTRACE integrates flexible cellular stoichiometry (e.g., N:C and Chl:C quotas), carbon allocation strategies, and evolving life-history traits within a multi-trait adaptive landscape. The goal is to understand how physiological plasticity and heritable evolution interact to reshape trait tradeoffs, population dynamics, and ecosystem function under environmental variability.

In parallel, I contributed to the development of a genome-scale metabolic modeling framework to classify marine heterotrophic bacteria by functional metabolic strategy rather than taxonomy. Using thousands of genome-scale metabolic models, we identified eight emergent metabolic guilds spanning fast-growing generalists, slow-growing specialists, and intermediate strategies. This work expands beyond the traditional copiotroph–oligotroph dichotomy and provides a functional basis for linking microbial diversity to biogeochemical processes at regional and global scales.

Across these projects, I aim to build predictive frameworks that connect microbial physiology and ecology to large-scale ocean patterns and processes.
